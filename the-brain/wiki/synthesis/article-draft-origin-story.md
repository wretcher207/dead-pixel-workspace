# I Taught Myself to Code Through Claude. Here's What That Actually Looked Like.

I didn't go to school for this. No bootcamp. No CS degree. No uncle who worked at Google. A year and a half ago I was an audio engineer making death metal records in a basement in Maine. Today I build production websites for paying clients, write custom tools, and run a web development business called Dead Pixel Design.

The thing that got me from point A to point B was an AI. Specifically, Claude.

I know how that sounds. Stick with me.

## The Before

I'm a musician. I play in a death metal band called Wretcher. I've spent most of my adult life in front of a DAW, not an IDE. My relationship with computers was deep but narrow. I knew my way around Reaper, MIDI, Neural DSP plugins. I could do things in a recording session that most people couldn't follow. But code? HTML was about as far as it went, and even that was rusty.

I'd tried to learn before. Watched some YouTube tutorials. Got about three videos into a Python course before the monotone instructor put me to sleep. The problem was never intelligence. The problem was that traditional learning assumes you're building toward some abstract future project. I don't work that way. I need to build something real, right now, and figure it out as I go.

## The Turn

I started using Claude the way most people do. Asking questions. Getting answers. The usual chatbot thing. But then I found Claude Code, which is a different animal entirely. It's not a chat window. It runs in the terminal. It reads your files, writes code, runs commands, and builds things alongside you.

The first time I gave it a project description and watched it scaffold out a Next.js application, create the component structure, write the Tailwind styles, and explain every decision it made, something clicked. This wasn't a search engine. This was a collaborator.

I didn't learn to code and then use Claude to speed things up. I learned to code *by using Claude*. Every project taught me something because Claude would write the code, and then I'd read it, break it, ask why it did what it did, and rebuild it. It's like learning a language by having a conversation instead of memorizing verb tables.

## What It Actually Looked Like

I'm not going to tell you it was smooth. It wasn't. Early on I'd accept whatever Claude gave me without understanding it. That bit me more than once. I'd ship something, a client would ask for a change, and I'd realize I had no idea why the code was structured the way it was.

The fix was simple but required discipline: stop copy-pasting blindly and start reading. Every function Claude wrote, I'd ask it to explain. Every architectural decision, I'd push back and ask for alternatives. Over time, the balance shifted. I went from "build me a navbar" to "here's how I want the navigation state to work, implement it this way."

Here's the part nobody tells you about learning through AI: it's not faster because it's easier. It's faster because you skip the artificial curriculum and go straight to building real things. You still have to put in the work. You still have to understand what's happening. The difference is you're learning by doing, not by watching someone else do it on YouTube.

## What I Build Now

Today my stack is Next.js, Tailwind CSS, deployed on Netlify through GitHub. I use Claude Code as my daily driver. Hooks, slash commands, MCP integrations, parallel agents. It's my whole development environment.

I build client websites. Custom tools. Internal apps. Things that would have taken a small agency and a real budget two years ago. I do it from Houlton, Maine, and I do it well. Not because I'm some prodigy. Because I found a tool that matched the way I actually learn, and I used it until I knew what I was doing.

The sites I build don't look or feel like AI slop. I care about design. I care about whether something feels right when you land on it. Claude handles the implementation. The vision is mine.

## Why I'm Telling You This

I set up Claude for small business owners now. That's one of the things Dead Pixel Design does. And the reason I'm good at it is because I've been on both sides. I know what it's like to stare at a terminal and feel completely lost. I also know what it looks like when the tools are configured right and everything clicks.

But there's a difference between asking a chatbot questions and having an AI agent that actually works inside your business. The gap between those two things is setup. That's the part nobody bothers to explain, and it's the part that makes all the difference.
