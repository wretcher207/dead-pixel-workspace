## What is Claude Cowork (and why should you care)?

![](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=UAmKyyZ-b9E)

Cowork is a separate tab inside the Claude Desktop app where Claude stops being a chatbot and starts being an agent.

You point it at a folder on your computer, describe what you want done, and it works through it on its own. Reading files, creating new ones, browsing the web, connecting to your tools. While you do something else.

The [origin story](https://claude.com/blog/cowork-research-preview) is actually pretty wild. When Anthropic released [Claude Code](https://aiblewmymind.substack.com/p/claude-code-subagents-explained) (their coding agent for developers), something unexpected happened. Developers used it for code, obviously, but they also started using it for [everything else](https://x.com/claudeai/status/2009666254815269313?s=20). And non-technical people saw what it could do and wanted in.

The problem? The terminal interface was a wall for anyone who wasn’t a developer.

So Anthropic’s Claude Code team, led by [Boris Cherny](https://x.com/bcherny?lang=en), used Claude Code itself to build Cowork in 10 days. The AI coding agent literally built its own non-technical sibling.

![Boris Cherny, Anthropic's head of Claude Code, confirming on X that Claude Code wrote all of Cowork's code, replying "All of it" to Alex Volkov's question about how much was AI-generated.](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Ww2u!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F11c4edf3-e210-4b11-9184-5110e1c3a8d9_1280x720.png)

Boris Cherny, Anthropic's head of Claude Code, confirming on X that Claude Code wrote all of Cowork's code, replying "All of it" to Alex Volkov's question about how much was AI-generated.

It [launched](https://x.com/claudeai/status/2010805682434666759?s=20) January 12, 2026 as a research preview for Max subscribers. Four days later, Pro users got access. A week after that, Team and Enterprise. Then [Plugins](https://claude.com/plugins) on January 30. [Windows](https://x.com/claudeai/status/2021336313979625910?s=20) on February 10. [Scheduled tasks](https://aiblewmymind.substack.com/p/claude-cowork-scheduled-tasks-6-ways) on February 25, so Claude can run recurring work on its own clock. And most recently, [computer use and Dispatch](https://aiblewmymind.substack.com/p/claude-computer-use-guide-6-workflows-tested), where Claude can control your screen, open apps, and work across your desktop, even when you’re not sitting in front of it.

Every two weeks, Anthropic ships something new. That's how fast this moves.

## When to use Cowork instead of regular Claude

I’ve seen a lot of people on Reddit asking why they’d use Cowork when so much can already be done in regular Claude. Fair question.

![Reddit user questioning why they'd use Claude Cowork over regular Claude, saying everything described could already be done with existing skills for content creation and data analysis.](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!VZBN!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fbcf8ba55-fc99-4836-bd26-7bad195fc644_1279x478.png)

Reddit user questioning why they'd use Claude Cowork over regular Claude, saying everything described could already be done with existing skills for content creation and data analysis.

These are the signals that tell me a task belongs in Cowork:

1. **The work is too big for a chat window.** Regular Claude has a limited context window. Push it too hard and it starts looping on retries or losing track of what you said. Cowork works locally in your folder, so it can read dozens of files, create much larger outputs, and run for extended periods without breaking down.
2. **The task has multiple steps and you don’t want to babysit each one.** “ *Take these meeting transcripts, extract action items, organize them by person, create a tracking spreadsheet, and draft follow-up emails* ”. In regular chat, that’s five separate prompts. In Cowork, you describe the outcome and come back to finished work.
3. **You need it to work directly with your files.** Regular Claude needs you to upload files and download outputs. Cowork reads and writes directly in your local folders. It creates actual Excel spreadsheets with working formulas, PowerPoint decks, formatted Word documents, ready to open and send.
4. **You need to combine your files with online research.** "Compare our pricing against 5 competitors and map it against our cost structure from this spreadsheet". With the Claude in Chrome extension, Cowork opens websites, scrolls through pages, extracts data, and combines what it finds with your local files in the same workflow.
5. **You’re doing something repetitive across many items.** Renaming 200 files. Processing 50 invoices. Generating reports for 15 essays. Describe the pattern once, Cowork applies it to everything.

## How to get started (and what most people miss)

You’ll need a [paid plan](https://claude.com/pricing) and the [Claude Desktop app](https://claude.com/download) (Mac or Windows).

![Claude Desktop app showing how to start a Cowork session by clicking the Cowork tab, selecting a folder, and typing a task description.](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!p1xW!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fc5f31e32-dd0b-4d13-bcbe-95c9f683f9df_1580x820.gif)

Claude Desktop app showing how to start a Cowork session by clicking the Cowork tab, selecting a folder, and typing a task description.

1. Once you open the app, **click “Cowork” at the top** (next to Chat and Code).
2. **Pick a folder** to give it access to (start with something non-sensitive).
3. You can start typing your task right away, but it's worth knowing about the **"+"** **button** — it lets you customize what Claude has access to:
- **Attach files** that aren’t in your selected folder but are relevant to the task.
- **Add connectors** to plug Claude into your external tools — Slack, Notion, Gmail, GitHub, and more. Once connected, Claude can read your calendar, pull Slack messages, or create Notion pages as part of the workflow.
- **Include a project** so it has access to the custom instructions and context you’ve already set up in your Claude Projects.
- **Use plugins**: pre-built bundles that turn Claude into a specialist for your role. You give one command and Claude runs the entire process on its own, then hands you the result. This is the agentic feature behind the "saaspocalypse". (Here's my [full breakdown](https://aiblewmymind.substack.com/p/claude-cowork-plugins-guide), where I shared what they are, how to use them, and how to create your own.)

And beyond the “+” button, there’s more you can do inside Cowork:

- **Schedule tasks:** Write the prompt once, use the /schedule command, and Claude Cowork runs it automatically, delivering the finished output for you to review. (I show how they work and how I used them to automate my own tasks in [this article](https://aiblewmymind.substack.com/p/claude-cowork-scheduled-tasks-6-ways).)
- **Let Claude use your computer:** Turn on screen control and Cowork can open apps, click buttons, type, and navigate your desktop on your behalf. Work side by side, or use Dispatch to send it tasks from your phone while you’re away from the computer. ([Here’s how to set it up and 6 workflows I tested.](https://aiblewmymind.substack.com/p/claude-computer-use-guide-6-workflows-tested))
- **Make Claude smarter with the [AI Blew My Mind MCP](https://mcp.aiblewmymind.com/):** Once connected, just talk to Claude about it. Ask it to show you everything inside the MCP, use a specific prompt, save your own recurring prompts, or generate images right in Cowork. No buttons, no menus. You just reference it in the conversation and Claude handles the rest. Takes one minute to set up. ([Here’s how, plus 5 examples of what you can do with it.](https://aiblewmymind.substack.com/p/claude-mcp-setup-prompts-images-workflows))

### Use case 1. Pet photo recognition and organization (a need coming from one of my paid subscribers)

**The problem:** This one came from a paid subscriber, Steve, who runs a pet sitting business. He has hundreds of pet photos with no organization. He wanted them identified by pet, sorted into a dedicated folder for each one, and renamed accordingly.

**What I did:** I set up a test folder with one reference photo per pet (labeled with the pet’s name) and a separate folder full of unsorted, mixed photos. Then I asked Cowork to analyze the reference photos, learn what each pet looks like, scan the unsorted folder, identify which pet is in each photo, create a subfolder for each pet, and move and rename every photo accordingly.

**What happened:** It correctly identified every pet in every photo and sorted them into the right folders. Now, my test was limited. I didn’t use as many photos as Steve would have, and I didn’t include multiple dogs of the same breed, which would be the real challenge. But for what I threw at it, it worked.

> **Verdict:** I'll definitely pass this to Steve to test with his actual collection and his own instructions. And if you work with ads and need to label content, or just need to organize a messy folder of local files, this could be really useful for you too.

### Use case 2. Turning my articles into Gumroad product pages

**The problem**: I have published [automations](https://aiblewmymind.substack.com/t/automations) and [prompts](https://aiblewmymind.substack.com/t/prompts-and-workflows) that could easily be digital products on [Gumroad](https://dariacupareanu.gumroad.com/). But turning those into sellable products means reformatting, writing sales copy, creating the listing, and packaging the deliverable.

**What I did**: Pointed Cowork at a folder with 9 of my articles and asked it to create Gumroad-ready product pages for each. Sales copy, pricing suggestions, product descriptions, and even formatted deliverables for some of them (like prompts, which I hadn't organized as neatly as my automations).

**What happened**: It did everything as asked. But it [hallucinated](https://aiblewmymind.substack.com/p/how-to-reduce-ai-hallucinations) in some parts, especially with automations that were similar to each other (like my [Inbox Manager](https://aiblewmymind.substack.com/p/the-inbox-manager-how-i-built-a-system) and these [vacation automations](https://aiblewmymind.substack.com/p/3-automations-i-built-to-make-returning) that also run on your inbox), so it needed human revision and corrections. It was 80% there though.

<video src="blob:https://aiblewmymind.substack.com/7158a6c1-c45d-49ce-9167-e608899208d0" controls=""></video>

> **Verdict:** Would use again, with review. The marketing copy, structure and formatting saved me at least a couple of hours. But don't publish without checking the details.

### Use case 3. Bank reconciliation, expense reporting, and invoice renaming

**The problem:** Every month I need to match my bank statement against invoices and figure out which ones are missing so I can send everything to my accountant. Half the platforms I pay for don't even send invoices via email, so I always forget some. It's one of those tasks I dread every single month.

**What I did:** I dropped my bank statement and just a few invoices into a folder and asked Cowork to do a full reconciliation. Analyze the bank statement, clean up vendor names, categorize each expense, match invoices to transactions, flag what’s missing, generate a formatted Excel file with everything, and rename the invoice files to a consistent format. All in one go.

**What happened:** Extracted everything correctly, renamed the invoices to a clean format, and marked all the transactions I didn’t provide an invoice for. (*I can’t show a video on this one for obvious privacy reasons, but it worked great.*)

![Claude Cowork bank reconciliation output showing an Excel spreadsheet with categorized expenses, matched invoices, and missing invoice rows highlighted in yellow.](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!9ujI!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F6041dcb5-0648-4d50-a263-d03171b0bd2f_1280x720.png)

### Use case 4. Content repurposing (articles into Substack notes spreadsheet)

**The problem:** Most of my published articles are evergreen, just as relevant six months later as the day they went out. But in practice, I only promote the recent ones. Going through older articles one by one to write fresh notes for each is the kind of task I keep putting off.

**What I did:** I gave Cowork access to a folder with all my articles and asked it to create a spreadsheet with 3 ready-to-post Substack notes for my 20 most recent ones. I didn’t even give details on the type of notes, because I knew it already had my substack-notes skill, [my writing skills](https://aiblewmymind.substack.com/p/claude-skills-ai-write-like-you), and a CSV with my past notes performance so it could analyze patterns and build on them.

<video src="blob:https://aiblewmymind.substack.com/2bfc0851-2140-4667-9fb3-fe6f0c115df1" controls=""></video>

**What happened:** It produced an Excel spreadsheet with 60 notes, as asked. It correctly identified the 20 most recent articles. And the notes are really good! [Here’s the Excel file it created, so you can see for yourself.](https://docs.google.com/spreadsheets/d/1OPIOCR8S60RHg8ITBuVIERyjWobxpc3E/edit?gid=687009210#gid=687009210)

> **Verdict:** Great system for content repurposing or any form of content creation. Would definitely repeat, and expand to other platforms too.
